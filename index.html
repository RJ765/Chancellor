Key Concepts Covered

Fiscal Policy (2.1.2a)

Government revenue and expenditure. Direct and indirect taxes. Budget deficits and surpluses.

Click to expand ▾
  • Direct taxes (income tax, corporation tax) — paid directly to HMRC; reduce disposable income → lower AD
  • Indirect taxes (VAT) — paid on spending; raise prices → cost-push inflation
  • Government expenditure — spending on public services, welfare, infrastructure → raises AD
  • Fiscal deficit = spending exceeds revenue; surplus = revenue exceeds spending
Exam tip: "Evaluate" questions on fiscal policy (6 marks) require you to weigh up expansionary vs contractionary effects, consider time lags, and reach a supported judgement.

Monetary Policy (2.1.2b)

Interest rate changes by the central bank. Transmission mechanism. Quantitative easing.

Click to expand ▾
  • Interest rates — set by the BoE MPC; higher rates → more expensive borrowing → lower consumer spending and investment → lower AD
  • Transmission mechanism — the chain from rate change → borrowing costs → spending → AD → output and prices
  • QE (asset purchasing) — central bank buys government bonds → injects money → lowers long-term rates → boosts AD
  • Higher rates also attract foreign capital → GBP appreciates (link to 2.2.3)
Exam tip: Always explain the transmission mechanism step by step. "Explain" (4 marks) = point + development for each step in the chain.

Supply-Side Policy (2.1.2c)

Education, infrastructure, privatisation, deregulation. Lagged effects on LRAS.

Click to expand ▾
  • Education & training → improved human capital → higher productivity → LRAS shifts right (lagged 3 quarters)
  • Infrastructure → lower costs of production → LRAS shifts right (lagged 2 quarters)
  • Privatisation → increased competition → lower prices, improved efficiency
  • Lower business taxes → higher post-tax profits → higher investment
  • Labour market reform → reduces structural unemployment
Exam tip: Supply-side policies are long-run — always mention time lags as a limitation. Examiners reward candidates who distinguish between short-run AD effects and long-run LRAS effects.

Objective Trade-offs (2.1.3)

Unemployment vs inflation. Growth vs environment. Inflation vs current account.

Click to expand ▾
  • Unemployment vs inflation — expansionary policy lowers unemployment but risks demand-pull inflation
  • Growth vs inflation — rapid growth overheats the economy, pulling up prices
  • Growth vs environment — industrial expansion and infrastructure damage the natural environment
  • Inflation vs current account — high domestic inflation makes exports less competitive → current account worsens
Exam tip: Trade-off questions are almost always AO4 "Evaluate" — you must present both sides and make a judgement. Use phrases like "however", "on the other hand", and "on balance".

Exchange Rates (2.2.3)

Appreciation and depreciation. Impact on imports, exports, and the current account.

Click to expand ▾
  • Appreciation → imports cheaper, exports more expensive → current account worsens; lower import-push inflation
  • Depreciation → imports more expensive (cost-push inflation), exports cheaper → current account improves
  • Interest rate link — higher rates attract hot money inflows → demand for GBP rises → appreciation
  • Exchange rates are determined by supply and demand for the currency in foreign exchange markets
Exam tip: Always link exchange rate changes back to the current account. The chain is: rate change → currency change → import/export price change → current account effect.

International Trade (2.2.2)

Free trade vs protectionism. Tariffs, quotas, and the role of the WTO.

Click to expand ▾
  • Free trade advantages — greater choice, lower prices, specialisation, economies of scale
  • Free trade disadvantages — structural unemployment, over-reliance on imports, infant industry vulnerability
  • Tariffs — taxes on imports; raise price, reduce quantity imported, protect domestic producers
  • Quotas — physical limits on import quantities
  • WTO — promotes free trade, resolves disputes, reduces trade barriers between member states
Exam tip: Protectionism questions often ask you to "Assess" — weigh up short-run domestic gains against long-run efficiency losses and risk of retaliation.

📖 Revision Reference Card

2.1.1 — Macroeconomic Objectives

  • Economic growth — measured by real GDP (total value of goods and services produced); economic cycle: boom → downturn → recession → recovery. Positive output gap = actual GDP > trend GDP. Negative output gap = spare capacity.
  • Low & stable inflation — measured by CPI (Consumer Price Index); BoE target 2% ±1%; demand-pull inflation (excess AD) vs cost-push inflation (rising input costs). Hyperinflation erodes purchasing power and distorts price signals.
  • Low unemployment — ILO definition: willing and able to work but without a job. Types: cyclical (demand-deficient), structural (mismatch of skills), seasonal, frictional (between jobs), voluntary (choose not to work at current wage).
  • Current account balance — trade in goods (visibles) + trade in services (invisibles) + primary income + secondary income. Persistent deficit → rising foreign debt, depreciation pressure.
  • Environmental protection — negative externalities of production (pollution, carbon emissions). Trade-off: faster growth may increase environmental damage.
  • Redistribution of income — progressive taxation + transfer payments reduce inequality. Gini coefficient measures income distribution. Absolute poverty vs relative poverty.

2.1.2a — Fiscal Policy

  • Revenue: direct taxes (income tax, corporation tax, NI) and indirect taxes (VAT, excise duties). Progressive taxes take a larger % from higher earners.
  • Expenditure: current spending (day-to-day: NHS, welfare, pensions) and capital spending (long-term: infrastructure, schools, hospitals).
  • Fiscal deficit = G > T; fiscal surplus = T > G. National debt = cumulative total of past deficits.
  • Expansionary: ↑G or ↓T → ↑AD → ↑GDP, ↑CPI, ↓unemployment, ↑deficit. Multiplier effect amplifies initial injection.
  • Contractionary: ↓G or ↑T → ↓AD → ↓GDP, ↓CPI, ↑unemployment, ↓deficit.
  • Limitations: time lags (recognition, implementation, effect); crowding out (government borrowing raises interest rates); political constraints (election cycles).

2.1.2b — Monetary Policy

  • Interest rates set by BoE Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) — 9 members, meets 8 times per year.
  • Transmission mechanism: ↑rates → ↑cost of borrowing → ↓consumer spending (mortgages, credit) → ↓business investment → ↓AD → ↓inflation.
  • ↓rates: → ↓cost of borrowing → ↑consumer spending → ↑business investment → ↑AD → ↑inflation, ↑growth.
  • QE: BoE purchases government bonds → ↑bond prices → ↓long-term yields → ↑money supply → ↑lending → ↑AD. Risk: asset price inflation.
  • Exchange rate link: ↑UK rates → ↑demand for £ (hot money) → £ appreciates → cheaper imports, dearer exports.
  • Taylor Rule: r* = 2 + 1.5×(π−2) + 0.5×(y−2) where π = CPI, y = GDP growth. Provides a benchmark for the appropriate interest rate.
  • Limitations: liquidity trap (rates near 0 lose effectiveness); time lags (6–18 months); blunt instrument (affects all sectors equally).

2.1.2c — Supply-Side Policies

  • Market-based: privatisation (state → private ownership), deregulation (remove barriers to entry), lower corporation tax (↑post-tax profit → ↑investment), labour market reform (reduce union power, ↓minimum wage).
  • Interventionist: education & training (↑human capital → ↑productivity, lagged), infrastructure (↓transport costs → ↑productive capacity, lagged), R&D subsidies (↑innovation).
  • Effect on diagrams: LRAS shifts right → ↑potential output → ↑GDP, ↓CPI (long run). PPC shifts outward.
  • Limitations: long time lags (education: 5–10 years); expensive (infrastructure billions); uncertain outcomes; may increase inequality short-term.

2.1.3 — Trade-offs Between Objectives

ConflictExplanation
Growth vs InflationRapid ↑AD → demand-pull inflation. Phillips Curve shows inverse short-run relationship between unemployment and inflation.
Growth vs EnvironmentHigher output → more pollution, resource depletion. Infrastructure may damage ecosystems.
Growth vs Current Account↑AD → ↑imports (higher income → consumers buy more imports) → CA worsens.
Low Unemployment vs InflationNear full employment → ↑wages → cost-push inflation. Central bank faces dilemma.
Inflation vs Current Account↑UK prices → UK exports less competitive → ↓exports, ↑imports → CA worsens.

2.2.2 — International Trade

  • Free trade: no tariffs/quotas → specialisation based on comparative advantage → ↑efficiency, ↓prices, ↑consumer choice.
  • Protectionism: tariffs (tax on imports), quotas (limit import volume), subsidies (support domestic producers), non-tariff barriers (regulations, standards).
  • Arguments for protectionism: protect infant industries, safeguard jobs, national security, prevent dumping, correct CA deficit.
  • Arguments against: retaliation (trade wars), inefficiency, higher prices for consumers, reduced choice, misallocation of resources.
  • MNCs: invest via FDI → create jobs, transfer technology, tax revenue. But: profit repatriation, exploitation of labour, environmental damage.

2.2.3 — Exchange Rates

  • Floating: determined by supply and demand for the currency in forex markets.
  • Appreciation (£ ↑ value) → imports cheaper, exports dearer → ↓CPI (cheaper imports), CA worsens (↓exports, ↑imports). Caused by: ↑interest rates, ↑demand for UK exports, ↑FDI, speculation.
  • Depreciation (£ ↓ value) → imports dearer (cost-push ↑CPI), exports cheaper → CA improves (↑exports, ↓imports). Caused by: ↓interest rates, ↓confidence, CA deficit, ↓FDI.
  • Marshall-Lerner condition: depreciation improves CA only if PED(X) + PED(M) > 1. J-curve effect: CA worsens initially before improving.
  • Hot money: short-term capital flows attracted by higher interest rates → ↑demand for £ → appreciation.
